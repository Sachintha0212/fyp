About Centella Asiatica

Centella Asiatica, known as Gotu Kola (Gotukola) in Sri Lanka, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to Asian wetlands. Revered for centuries in Ayurvedic and traditional medicine, it is rich in triterpenoid compounds with exceptional therapeutic value.

Scientific Name
Centella Asiatica (L.) Urban
Family
Apiaceae (Umbelliferae)
Native Region
Asia & Pacific Islands

Key Characteristics

  • Small fan-shaped leaves with scalloped edges
  • Thrives in moist, shaded environments
  • Rich in asiaticoside, madecassoside, and asiatic acid
  • Used for wound healing, cognitive enhancement, and skin repair
  • Optimal temperature: 20–30°C, humidity: 60–80%
  • Spreads via stolons — ground-covering growth habit

Pest & Disease

Common Diseases & Pests

Identifying and managing threats to Centella Asiatica cultivation.

Red Spider Mite

Pest Infestation

Description

Tetranychus urticae — Tiny arachnids (0.5 mm) that feed on plant sap. Barely visible to the naked eye, appearing as red or brown moving dots on leaf undersides.

Symptoms

  • Fine silky webbing on leaf undersides
  • Yellow or white stippling on leaf surfaces
  • Leaves turn bronze, crispy, and dry
  • Severe infestation causes defoliation
  • Stunted overall growth

Treatment

  • Apply neem oil solution (1-2%) weekly
  • Use miticides: abamectin or bifenazate
  • Introduce predatory mites (Phytoseiidae)
  • Increase humidity above 60% to deter mites
  • Strong water spray to dislodge mites

Cercospora Leaf Spot

Fungal Disease

Description

Cercospora centellae — A fungal pathogen causing characteristic circular spots on foliage. Spreads via airborne spores in humid, warm conditions above 25°C.

Symptoms

  • Small circular spots with dark brown borders
  • Grey or tan necrotic centers
  • Yellow halo surrounding each spot
  • Spots coalesce, causing large dead areas
  • Premature leaf drop in severe cases

Treatment

  • Apply copper-based fungicides preventively
  • Mancozeb or chlorothalonil sprays
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves
  • Improve air circulation around plants
  • Avoid overhead irrigation; water at base

Pythium Root Rot

Oomycete Disease

Description

Pythium spp. — A water mould thriving in waterlogged, poorly drained soils. Attacks roots and crown tissue, causing rapid plant decline.

Symptoms

  • Dark brown, mushy root tissue
  • Wilting despite adequate moisture
  • Yellowing of lower leaves progressing upward
  • Foul smell from infected roots
  • Stem base turns soft and discoloured

Treatment

  • Improve drainage; avoid waterlogging
  • Apply metalaxyl or fosetyl-aluminium
  • Use Trichoderma-based biocontrol agents
  • Sterilize planting media before use
  • Raise beds to improve aeration

Aphid Infestation

Pest Infestation

Description

Aphis gossypii and related species — Soft-bodied sap-sucking insects that cluster on new growth. Also vectors for viral plant diseases.

Symptoms

  • Curling or puckering of young leaves
  • Sticky honeydew deposits on leaf surfaces
  • Black sooty mold on honeydew residue
  • Distorted, stunted new growth
  • Visible colonies on stem tips

Treatment

  • Apply insecticidal soap or neem oil
  • Introduce ladybirds or lacewings
  • Imidacloprid systemic insecticide
  • Strong water jets to wash off colonies
  • Remove heavily infested shoot tips
Nutrition

Nutrient Deficiencies

Recognizing and correcting nutritional imbalances in Centella Asiatica.

N
Nitrogen Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Uniform yellowing of older (lower) leaves first
  • Pale green to yellow color on entire plant
  • Reduced leaf size and slow growth rate
  • Thin, spindly stems with few stolons

Correction

Apply urea (46-0-0) at 100–150 kg N/ha. Organic options: compost tea, blood meal, chicken manure. Foliar urea spray (2%) for quick uptake.

P
Phosphorus Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Dark green or purple/red tint on older leaves
  • Purple coloration on leaf undersides
  • Poor root development and stolon spread
  • Delayed maturity and reduced yield

Correction

Apply TSP or DAP fertilizer. pH should be 6.0–7.0 for best P availability. Mycorrhizal inoculants enhance phosphorus uptake significantly.

K
Potassium Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Scorched or burned leaf margins
  • Yellowing and browning of leaf edges
  • Weak stems prone to lodging
  • Increased susceptibility to disease

Correction

Apply muriate of potash (KCl) or potassium sulfate. Organic sources include wood ash and compost. Apply 100–150 kg K₂O/ha.

H₂O
Water Stress

Visual Symptoms

  • Drought: Wilting, leaf curl, crispy brown margins
  • Waterlogging: Yellow leaves, soft mushy stems
  • Root rot symptoms (dark roots, foul odour)
  • Reduced leaf size and premature drop

Management

Maintain 50–70% field capacity. Use drip irrigation, water daily in hot weather, ensure good drainage, and apply organic mulch to retain moisture.

Mg
Magnesium Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins)
  • Veins remain green while leaf tissue yellows
  • Symptoms appear on older leaves first
  • Reddish-purple tints in severe cases

Correction

Apply Epsom salt (MgSO₄) as foliar spray (2%) or soil drench. Dolomitic lime for long-term correction. 10–20 kg Mg/ha soil application.

Fe
Iron Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Interveinal chlorosis on young leaves first
  • New growth appears yellow-white
  • Veins remain distinctly green
  • Severe cases: complete leaf whitening

Correction

Apply chelated iron (Fe-EDTA) as foliar spray (0.5–1%) or iron sulfate to soil. Lower pH to 6.0–6.5 for better iron availability. Add organic matter.
