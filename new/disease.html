About Centella Asiatica

Centella Asiatica, commonly known as Gotu Kola or Gotukola in Sri Lanka, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to Asian wetlands. It has been used for centuries in traditional medicine and is highly valued for its medicinal properties.

Scientific Name

Centella Asiatica (L.) Urban

Family

Apiaceae (Umbelliferae)

Origin

Asia, Pacific Islands

Key Characteristics

  • Small, fan-shaped leaves with scalloped edges
  • Grows in moist, shaded environments
  • Rich in triterpenoid compounds (asiaticoside, madecassoside)
  • Used for wound healing, cognitive enhancement, and skin health
  • Thrives in temperatures between 20-30°C
  • Requires high humidity (60-80%) and adequate moisture

Common Diseases & Pests

Understanding and identifying diseases affecting Centella Asiatica

Red Spider Mite

Pest Infestation

Description

Tetranychus urticae - Tiny arachnids that feed on plant sap, causing significant damage to Centella Asiatica leaves. They are barely visible to the naked eye (0.5mm) and appear as red or brown dots.

Symptoms

  • Fine webbing on undersides of leaves
  • Yellow or white stippling on leaf surfaces
  • Leaves become bronzed and dry
  • Severe infestations cause leaf drop
  • Stunted plant growth

Conditions

Thrives in hot, dry conditions. Most active when temperature exceeds 27°C and humidity is below 50%. Rapid reproduction (new generation every 5-20 days).

Prevention & Treatment

  • Maintain high humidity (60-80%)
  • Regular water spraying on leaf undersides
  • Introduce predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis)
  • Use neem oil spray (organic treatment)
  • Apply insecticidal soap
  • Remove heavily infested leaves
  • Avoid water stress - keep soil consistently moist

Leaf Spot Disease

Fungal Infection

Description

Caused by various fungal pathogens including Cercospora and Alternaria species. Common in humid conditions with poor air circulation.

Symptoms

  • Circular brown or black spots on leaves
  • Yellow halos around spots
  • Spots may merge to form larger necrotic areas
  • Premature leaf yellowing and drop
  • Reduced plant vigor

Conditions

Favored by excessive moisture on leaves, high humidity (>85%), poor air circulation, and temperatures between 20-28°C. Spreads rapidly during rainy seasons.

Prevention & Treatment

  • Ensure proper plant spacing for air circulation
  • Water at soil level, avoid wetting leaves
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves immediately
  • Apply copper-based fungicides
  • Use organic fungicides (neem oil, baking soda solution)
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
  • Practice crop rotation

Root Rot

Fungal Infection

Description

Caused by soil-borne pathogens like Pythium, Phytophthora, and Rhizoctonia. Results from waterlogged soil conditions and poor drainage.

Symptoms

  • Wilting despite adequate soil moisture
  • Yellowing and drooping leaves
  • Dark, mushy roots with foul odor
  • Stunted growth and plant collapse
  • Brown discoloration at base of stems

Conditions

Develops in waterlogged, poorly drained soils with insufficient oxygen. Excessive irrigation, heavy clay soils, and poor drainage are primary causes.

Prevention & Treatment

  • Ensure excellent soil drainage
  • Avoid overwatering - monitor soil moisture
  • Use raised beds in heavy soils
  • Apply biological fungicides (Trichoderma)
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Improve soil structure with organic matter
  • Sterilize soil before replanting

Powdery Mildew

Fungal Infection

Description

White, powdery fungal growth caused by various species including Erysiphe and Oidium. Common in moderate temperatures with high humidity.

Symptoms

  • White, powdery coating on leaves and stems
  • Distorted or curled leaves
  • Yellowing and premature leaf drop
  • Reduced photosynthesis and plant vigor
  • Stunted growth in severe cases

Prevention & Treatment

  • Provide adequate spacing for air flow
  • Spray with sulfur-based fungicides
  • Apply baking soda solution (1 tbsp/gallon water)
  • Use neem oil as preventive measure
  • Remove infected plant parts
  • Avoid overhead watering in evening

Nutrient Deficiencies

Identifying and correcting nutritional disorders in Centella Asiatica

N

Nitrogen Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Overall yellowing (chlorosis) of older leaves first
  • Stunted growth and reduced plant size
  • Pale green to yellow-green coloration
  • Premature senescence of lower leaves
  • Reduced leaf production

Impact on Growth

Nitrogen is essential for protein synthesis, chlorophyll formation, and overall vegetative growth. Deficiency severely limits photosynthesis and biomass production.

Correction Methods

  • Organic: Compost, well-rotted manure, blood meal
  • Inorganic: Urea (46-0-0), Ammonium sulfate (21-0-0)
  • Application: 50-100 kg N/ha depending on soil test
  • Foliar spray: 1-2% urea solution for quick correction
  • Timing: Split applications during active growth
P

Phosphorus Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Dark green to purplish discoloration of leaves
  • Purple or reddish tints on leaf margins and stems
  • Stunted root development
  • Reduced tillering and branching
  • Delayed maturity and flowering
  • Necrotic spots on older leaves

Impact on Growth

Phosphorus is critical for energy transfer (ATP), root development, and metabolic processes. Deficiency leads to poor root systems, reducing nutrient and water uptake capacity.

Correction Methods

  • Organic: Bone meal, rock phosphate, composted manure
  • Inorganic: Triple superphosphate (0-46-0), DAP (18-46-0)
  • Application: 30-60 kg P₂O₅/ha based on soil analysis
  • Soil pH: Maintain 6.0-7.0 for optimal P availability
  • Placement: Band application near roots for efficiency
  • Mycorrhizae: Beneficial fungi enhance P uptake
K

Potassium Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Yellowing (chlorosis) of leaf margins and tips
  • Necrotic (brown, dead) edges on older leaves
  • Leaf scorching and curling
  • Weak, thin stems prone to lodging
  • Reduced disease resistance
  • Interveinal chlorosis progressing to necrosis

Impact on Growth

Potassium regulates water balance, enzyme activation, and stress tolerance. Deficiency weakens cell walls, reduces drought tolerance, and increases susceptibility to diseases and pests.

Correction Methods

  • Organic: Wood ash, kelp meal, green sand
  • Inorganic: Muriate of potash (0-0-60), Sulfate of potash (0-0-50)
  • Application: 40-80 kg K₂O/ha depending on deficiency severity
  • Foliar spray: 2% K₂SO₄ solution for rapid response
  • Timing: Apply during active growth periods
  • Soil test: Monitor K levels regularly
H₂O

Water Stress

Visual Symptoms

  • Drought Stress: Wilting, leaf curling, crispy brown edges
  • Yellowing and premature leaf drop
  • Reduced leaf size and slow growth
  • Overwatering: Yellowing, soft mushy stems
  • Root rot symptoms (foul odor, dark roots)
  • Fungal diseases in waterlogged conditions

Impact on Growth

Centella Asiatica requires consistently moist soil. Water stress disrupts nutrient uptake, photosynthesis, and cellular functions. Both drought and waterlogging are detrimental.

Optimal Water Management

  • Soil moisture: Maintain 50-70% field capacity
  • Irrigation: Drip irrigation or light sprinklers preferred
  • Frequency: Water daily or every 2 days in hot weather
  • Drainage: Ensure proper drainage to prevent waterlogging
  • Mulching: Apply organic mulch to retain moisture
  • Monitoring: Use soil moisture sensors for precision
  • Timing: Water in early morning to reduce disease risk
Mg

Magnesium Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins)
  • Veins remain green while tissue yellows
  • Symptoms appear on older leaves first
  • Leaf margins may curl upward
  • Reddish-purple tints in severe cases

Correction Methods

  • Organic: Epsom salt (MgSO₄), dolomitic lime
  • Foliar spray: 2% Epsom salt solution
  • Soil application: 10-20 kg Mg/ha
  • pH adjustment: Lime application if soil is acidic
Fe

Iron Deficiency

Visual Symptoms

  • Interveinal chlorosis on young leaves
  • New growth appears yellow or white
  • Veins remain green (distinct pattern)
  • Severe cases: complete leaf whitening
  • Reduced growth and vigor

Correction Methods

  • Chelated iron: Fe-EDTA foliar spray (0.5-1%)
  • Soil application: Iron sulfate (FeSO₄)
  • pH management: Lower pH to 6.0-6.5 for better Fe availability
  • Organic matter: Compost improves Fe uptake
